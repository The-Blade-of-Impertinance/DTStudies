Thermoforming (Thermoplastics)

Polymers that soften with heat and can be reshaped repeatedly

Definition

Thermoforming refers to heating a thermoplastic sheet until it becomes pliable, then shaping it over a mould and cooling it to retain that shape. The plastic can be reheated and reshaped multiple times without permanent chemical change.

Key properties

Reversible — heat softens, cooling hardens, repeatedly. Recyclable — can be melted down and reprocessed. Generally flexible and impact resistant. Softens at moderate temperatures.

Thermosetting plastics

Polymers that permanently harden through an irreversible chemical reaction

Definition

Thermosetting polymers undergo a permanent chemical change (cross-linking) when heated during moulding. Once set, the molecular structure is fixed — they cannot be remelted, recycled, or reshaped.

Key properties

Irreversible — the chemical change is permanent. Hard and rigid once set. Excellent heat resistance — maintains shape under high temperatures. Typically brittle under stress.

Vacuum forming process

How thermoforming is used to shape plastic sheets over a mould

Overview

Vacuum forming is a thermoforming technique used to shape thermoplastic sheets. It uses heat + atmospheric pressure (via vacuum) to pull softened plastic around a mould.

1

Heat the sheet

A thermoplastic sheet is clamped in a frame and heated until soft and pliable — typically 160–180°C for acrylic (PMMA).

2

Position the mould

A rigid mould (hard tool) is placed below the softened sheet. Its shape determines the final form of the product.

3

Apply vacuum

Air is evacuated from below, pulling the soft plastic tightly around every contour of the mould using atmospheric pressure.

4

Cool & remove

The plastic cools and rigidifies, retaining the mould's shape. It is then released and trimmed to produce the finished component.

Typical applications

Packaging trays, product enclosures, baths & shower trays, car dashboards, chocolate box inserts, and school DT projects.
